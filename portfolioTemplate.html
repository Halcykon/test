CHALLENGE

It is expensive to keep datasets accurate and updated.

Petrophysicists described the data validation process as "herding cats in the dark".

Just when they thought they compiled all their drilling reports, a new dataset would appear and invalidate previous work. Teams kept discovering critical data errors late into projects, forcing costly rework.

RESEARCH PROCESS

How is data relayed from one user to the next? How is it transformed?

I needed to map how data flows from raw measurements to final geological reports. I organized research questions into four categories:

People — Who handles data at each stage?

Pain Points — Where do handoffs break down?

Priorities — What quick wins vs. transformative changes should we target?

Processes — How and why is data transformed?


RESEARCH METHODS

●    2 contextual inquiries (shadowed petrophysicists during active project) 

●    8 stakeholder interviews (data engineers, geologists, petrophysicists)

●    Data flow mapping workshop with 8 cross-functional team members


PARTICIPANTS

●    1 senior petrophysicist (15+ years experience)

●    2 junior petrophysicists (2-4 years experience)

●    3 senior geologists (for comparative workflow analysis)

●    2 data engineers (to understand technical constraints)

CRITICAL FINDINGS

Highlight Insight 1.

Unit conversion errors and mislabeled wells don't get caught until weeks into analysis. One junior petrophysicist told me about spending three weeks analyzing what she thought was Well A-12, only to discover it was mislabeled; it was actually B-09, in a completely different geological formation. She had to start over.

Why this matters: Catching these errors at ingestion could save at least 15-20 hours per project.

Highlight Insight 2.

Teams waste days waiting for data that is stuck in legal review, already exists elsewhere, or was never collected. There's no centralized system to check data status.

Why this matters: Visibility into the pipeline prevents false starts and allows teams to prioritize work intelligently.

"I've waited a month for a dataset only to discover it never made it out of the wellsite. No one told me."

NUMBERED LIST INSIGHT

Augment, not automate, data validation.
  1. Our original hypothesis was wrong. We assumed petrophysicists wanted us to automate data preparation so they could focus on analysis.
  2. Humans need to be in the loop. Petrophysicists need to stay involved in data prep because that's where they build intuition about data quality.
  3. How might we surface critical data at the right time? Instead of removing petrophysicists from data prep, we focused on making data preparation radically more efficient while preserving their ability to validate data.

DESIGN PRINCIPLE

Surface problems early,
provide tools to investigate,
and never hide the raw truth.

EXPLORED CONCEPTS

Concept A: Automated Data Validator
A system that runs validation rules and only surfaces "clean" datasets.

Rejected: Users felt this was a black box. They wanted to see what was validated and how, not just a green checkmark.
Concept B: Notification-Based System
Push alerts when new data arrives or errors are detected.

Rejected: Users are already drowning in emails. They needed a "pull" system they check when starting a project, not more interruptions.

INTERFACE DESIGN AND TESTING

Usability Testing Goals

I conducted three rounds of testing with 5 total users (3 petrophysicists, 2 geologists as proxies).

Success criteria

1. Users can identify data quality issues without reading documentation

2. Users can locate needed datasets within 30 seconds

3. Users understand what every UI element does without asking questions

Iteration 1: Map view vs. List view

Map view: 4/5 users found target datasets in 18-25 seconds

List view: 5/5 users took 45-90 seconds and expressed frustration


Issues found

●    Color coding was ambiguous ("what does red mean?")

●    Data age icons were too small to notice

●    Pipeline status was buried in a sidebar

FINAL DESIGN FEATURES

1. Map View Browser

Wells displayed on an interactive map with color-coded freshness indicators. Users can zoom to regions, filter by data type, and see availability at a glance.

Design rationale: Matches the spatial mental model petrophysicists already use. Reduces cognitive load by eliminating translation between geography and abstract lists.

2. Proactive Error Detection

Automated checks flag unit inconsistencies, labeling errors, and suspicious values at data ingestion. Errors appear as visual alerts on well markers.

Design rationale: Catches issues when they're cheapest to fix (at ingestion vs. mid-analysis). Visual alerts create immediate awareness without requiring active monitoring.

3. Transparent Data Lineage

Two-click access to raw measurements from any transformed dataset. Visual timeline shows transformation steps.

Design rationale: Preserves user trust by making the "sausage-making" visible. Enables validation without adding friction.

4. Pipeline Status Awareness

Left panel shows all data stages: what's collected, in-transit, stuck in approval, or available. "Expected Data" section shows gaps.

Design rationale: Eliminates the black box of data availability. Allows teams to plan around data arrival rather than waiting blindly.

5. Relevant Data Grouping Suggestions

Recommended related datasets (logs, cores, seismic) clustered by well and project. Users can create custom collections.

Design rationale: Reduces time spent hunting for related files. Mirrors how petrophysicists think about data relationships.

TECH CONSTRAINTS

Legacy Database Integration

Issue: ExxonMobil's data lived across 15+ disconnected systems, some dating to the 1980s. Real-time data freshness required building new APIs.

Solution: Based on user interviews, we prioritized the 5 most-used databases for MVP, leaving 10 for Phase 2.

Colorblind Accessibility

Issue: My "freshness" color system failed for colorblind users.

Solution: I added pattern overlays (solid/hatched/dotted) and text labels on hover and tested with 2 colorblind users to validate.

Offshore Connectivity

Issue: Field teams work on oil rigs with intermittent internet. They needed offline access but our dashboard required live data.

Solution: Phase 1 focused on onshore teams (75% of users). Phase 2 roadmap includes offline sync mode. I documented offline requirements for future designers.
