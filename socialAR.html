How Might AR Improve Networking?

How might augmented reality enhance the networking experience of finding and meeting relevant contacts?

Duration

3 months

Role

UX Research +
 Interaction Design

Organization

CMU Research Project

Team

Aaron Faucher
Ravi Morbia

CHALLENGE

How might Augmented Reality help strangers find common ground?

In networking events, people often approach strangers arbitrarily, often by proximity, gender, or age. What if we could surface deeper interests beyond superficial commonalities?

SOLUTION

Alert users of people of interest — sparingly.

Implementing AR networking is not a question of if it can be done, but how it can be done. From our user testing, we realized our biggest challenge is balancing the usefulness of AR without being too distracting. Our journey below presents some guidelines on implementation.

INITIAL EXPLORATION

How could AR facilitate professional networking? 

As computers weave themselves into the fabric of our lives, it is crucial to understand how technology can subtly blend with our attention. This study explores AR with the HoloLens 2, but with a designed eye towards future (lighter and wider field-of-view) AR technology.

What problem are we solving with AR, and is AR the right solution for this?

We wanted to make sure that our end product would be built on solid user data and research insight. With our focus on professional networking, we employed participatory observation and fly-on-the-wall methods to understand how people currently met each other.

Facilitators helped connect strangers through common interests. We also noted that a worn emblem, like a college hoodie, provided a talking point.

Well-organized events create opportunities to meet strangers by forcing proximity (lunch tables, booths, collaborative games)

PROTOTYPING

How does AR feel like?

We paper prototyped to cheaply test how AR might feel to users.

“Is that stranger creepily staring at me or reading my information?”

Before we prototyped with the HoloLens, we stuck cut post-its to our faces and pretended to be strangers. It was surprisingly informative and helped us generate a list of concerns surrounding data privacy, attention, and cognitive load.

For instance, body language becomes hard to decipher when individuals are experiencing custom-tailored realities. When we placed AR information on my forehead, it was unclear if someone was just “browsing” the room or wanted to talk to me.

We grouped our biggest questions around certain themes

IDEATION

When, how, and where does information appear?

Using Crazy Eights, ‘Yes And’ improv, and Round Robin, we ideated a variety of ideas to explore.

We shifted to focus on the experience before conversations happened.

After consolidating results from our early user tests, we focused on two areas of opportunity for AR: pre-approach and during conversation. However, we realized that our scope was still too wide. We found that applying AR during conversation was too variable.

USER TESTING

We ran a series of low fidelity tests and interviews on 17 graduate students.

We observed their reactions when data literally surrounded them, and how their social interactions were influenced by those data.

"Walk us through the last time you were at a networking event."

We relied on personal anecdotes to avoid data gathered from generalized feelings and hypothetical situations.

"A stranger comes up to you and says, 'Wow, those hydrangeas outside your front porch are my favorite kind of flowers!"

We also put our users in uncomfortable scenarios and explored edge cases when AR pushes against users' comfort zones.

We synthesized our results in an affinity diagram, which helped us better define the areas to focus on.

NARROWING THE SCOPE

We designed a matchmaking tag system.

As our prototype progressed, we narrowed to a matchmaking tag system that alerted the user if others had matching interest tags.

But I still want to see tags that don't match mine.

Low fidelity user tests invalidated some of our original assumptions. For example, we did not consider that users could use unmatched tags as icebreaker topics.

KEY QUESTIONS

  1. Privacy Comfort Zones: To what extent are users comfortable with sharing personal data?
  2. Attention Economy: How much information can we display without becoming too distracting?
  3. Contextual Awareness: Where are AR displays placed, and when would data be shared with other users?

USER STUDY

So does our system help users meet new people?

With our program running on HoloLens, we conducted a think-aloud user study with 5 graduate students from various industry backgrounds. We took video recordings, noted their verbal and non-verbal reactions, and conducted a retrospective interview after each think aloud.

Our results validated our prior low fidelity findings, and uncovered other opportunities for development.

REFLECTION

This endeavor was the first challenge I took exploring a novel technology. Since no design patterns (or even stable software releases!) existed, we were designing and hacking together pieces at every step. Classic design methods were useful to fall back on, but had to be adapted for this novel domain.

If I could continue this project, I would love to expand on the team-bonding concepts we found in research.
What if a member's interests determined what AR games they could play with others?
Or pre-selected their affiliation in an alternate reality game?
Could we even eliminate networking events as they are today, by allowing organizers to create improvised team games based on interest?
